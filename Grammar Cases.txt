Polish language uses cases, to give a certain meaning to words.

It can transform nouns, adjectives, numbers, pronouns.

READING THIS WON'T MAKE YOU MAGICALLY TRANSFORM WORDS CORRECTLY!!!
BY READING THIS *I HOPE* YOU WILL !UNDERSTAND! HOW THE CASES WORK!
So I hope after reading this, you won't say stuff like "Idę do szkoła."!


https://polszczyzna.pl/przypadki-w-jezyku-polskim/

The case names are as follows:

	Mianownik	(latin: nominativus)
	Dopełniacz	(latin: genetivus)
	Celownik	(latin: dativus)
	Biernik		(latin: accusativus)
	Narzędnik	(latin: instrumentalist)	
	Miejscownik	(latin: locativus)
	Wołacz		(latin: vocativus)

Now, let's get more into that.
Each case has its own set of questions that, once answered, will give a correct form.
This unfortunately, works mainly only for native speakers, as we are used to the all kind of word varieties, and once we ask a question in our head, we can find a matching form of a word.
(So good luck, foreigners!)

	Mianownik	Kto? Co? (Who? What [thing]?) 		
	Dopełniacz	Kogo? Czego? (Who? What?) 	
	Celownik	Komu? Czemu?(Who? What?)	
	Biernik		Kogo? Co?(Who? What [thing]?)
	Narzędnik	Z kim? Z czym? (With whom? With what[thing]?)
	Miejscownik	O kim? O czym? (About whom? About what[thing]?)
	Wołacz		No question, it's used to address someone - o!



Pretty crazy. So many forms but in English it's mostly Who and What.
It is just as non informative for a native, as it is for a foreigner (kind of)
Polish people use "question complements" to make the questions more distinct.
And so the questions are as follows:

	Mianownik	Kto jest? Co jest? 		(Who is? What is? = This is ...)
	Dopełniacz	Kogo nie ma? Czego nie ma?	(Who isn't [here]? What isn't [here]? = ... isn't here)
	Celownik	Komu to daje? Czemu* to daje?	(I give it to who? I give it to what?)
	Biernik		Kogo widzę? Co widzę?		(Who do I see? What do I see? = I see ...)
	Narzędnik	Z kim idę? Z czym idę?		(I go with whom? I go with what? = I go with ...)
	Miejscownik	O kim myślę? O czym myślę?	(I think about whom? I think about what?)
	Wołacz		o!				(examples: Piter! Come over here!. Piter will be in a "wołacz" form**)


* = Czemu doesn't mean "why?". In this particular meaning it means TO WHAT(kind of a thing) I give a book? for example.
** = Polish translation:	Piotrze! Chodź tutaj!
				Mianownik:	Piotr (Who is? Piotr is (here)). 
				Wołacz: 	Piotrze!


Time for some examples.
Please pay attention to the cases, not grammar per say.

Since I already started with a Polish male name, Piotr, I will use it as a first example.


	Mianownik	Piotr		Kto jest? Piotr jest [tuta]. 			Who is? Piotr is [here]
	Dopełniacz	Piotra		Kogo nie ma [tutaj]? Piotra nie ma [tutaj]. 	Who isn't [here]? Piotr isn't [here].*
	Celownik	Piotrowi	(reverse order)**Daję to komu?Daję to Piotrowi.	I give it to who? I give it to Piotr.
	Biernik		Piotra		(reverse)Widzę kogo? Widzę Piotra.		I see who? I see Piotr.
	Narzędnik	z Piotrem	(reverse)Idę z kim? Idę z Piotrem.		I go with whom? I go with Piotr.
	Miejscownik	o Piotrze	(reverse)Myślę o kim? Myślę o Piotrze.		I think about whom? I think about Piotr.
	Wołacz		Piotrze!***	


* = Ok, so you can think of Dopełniacz also in situations like " of Piotr "(of who?), or " Piotr's "(who's?)
	Piotr's car = Samochód Piotra
	Relative of Piotr = Krewny Piotra
	What is the age of Piotr's Sister? = Jaki jest wiek Piotra siostry.
		This is a very nice sentence. Let's break it down.
		
		Jaki jest wiek Piotra siostry.
		wiek	= mianownik	= age
		Piotra	= dopełniacz	= Piotr's 	(<-- Piotr's sister)
		Siostry = dopełniacz	= Sister's 	(<-- Sister's age)
		
		We can break it down ever further for better understanding.

		Jaki jest wiek siostry?	= Jaki jest wiek kogo?	= What is the age of who?
		Jaki jest wiek Piotra siostry? = Jaki jest wiek kogo siostry? = What is the age of whose sister?



** = To "match" English translation, I have reversed the order of the question. It IS allowed in Polish language, mainly BECAUSE we have cases.
	In case you wonder, the verbs are in first person form. I could also write it like that:
	Komu to daje?	= Daję to komu?	= Ja daję to komu?
	Kogo widzę?	= Widzę kogo?	= Ja widzę kogo?
	Z kim idę?	= Idę z kim?	= Ja idę z kim?
	O kim myślę?	= Myślę o kim?	= Ja myślę o kim?

	I reversed the order, to give an example similar to a chinese sentence, where I only replace the actual nouns, without changing any word order.
		他是Piotr = 他是谁？
		你喜欢和Piotr一起看电视 = 你喜欢和谁一起看电视？



*** = Well... As I said it is to adress someone. It goes out of use, as nowadays we just shout the name of a person we want to "draw attention" of.
	Piotr! Chodź tutaj! = Piotr! Come here! <- Mianownik used as Wołacz
	Piotrze! Chodź tutaj! = Piotr! Come here! <-Wołacz in action. Feels kind of old-fashion

Now that you had a glimpse on the cases, it's time to explain them more (I didn't want you to fall asleep at the very beginning).


	Mianownik	- This form of a word is used in dictionaries. It is the very base of a word - unchanged.
			- It is when the word is used as a Subject.

	Dopełniacz	- Used when talking that something belongs TO SOMEONE/SOMETHING. Similar to English "of someone" or "someone's". Similar to Chinese someone的
			- Used for a noun that is an object of a negative sentences. I did not eat the cake. Ja nie zjadłem tortu.
			- Used with numbers:	Five cats = pięć kotów.
						Show us the fifth cat = Pokaż nam piątego kota.

			- Used when saying that you go somewhere.
				Normally, the question could be "where?", but you should think of it as "to who? to what?"
					Idę do szkoły. - I go to school.
					Idę do kogo?/do czego? - I go to who(m)?/to what?
					Idę do szkoły(Dopełniacz)

	Celownik	- Is used mainly for situations that something is given TO SOMEONE. I gave the book to Piotr = Dałem książke Piotrowi.
			- Used with verbs such as (but not limited to)
						dawać		(give)
						mówić		(talk)
						sprzedawać	(sell)
						oddawać		(give back to/return)
						pomagać		(help)
						obiecywać	(promise)
						dziękować	(thank)

			- Used with prepositions such as: 
						dzięki		(thanks to) I did it thanks to you - Zrobiłem to dzięki tobie.
						przeciw 	(against to) Proposal against abortion - Wniosek przeciw aborcji.
						wbrew		(against to) Against my will - Wbrew mojej woli.
						ku		(???)	For the country's glory! - Ku chwale ojczyzny!
									To my surprise - Ku mojemu zdziwieniu.

	Biernik		- used for an object of a verb THAT ARE NOT NEGATED (If negated, they turn into Dopełniacz)
				Widzę (kogo/co?) kota.			I see a cat		Biernik
				Nie widzę (kogo/czego?) kota.		I don't see the cat	Dopełniacz(Not a good example, as both forms are the same)

				lubię (kogo?co?) muzykę.		I lubię music		Biernik
				Nie lubię (kogo? czego?) muzyki.	I don't lubię music	Dopełniacz

	Narzędnik	- Let's clear things out. The "z" part is optional. It matches the question With whom? Z kim? yet, once you get more advanced, you can just ask "kim?/czym?"
				It also joins with prepositions like:
							z kim? z czym?		with whom? with what?
							nad kim? nad czym?	above who? above what?
							pod kim? pod czym?	under who? under what?

				If we skip preposition, you can ask ALL KIND OF QUESTIONS.

				Describes tools that you use to complete an action:

					Wbijam gwoździe czym? Wbijam gwoździe młotkiem.	I hammer the nails with what? I hammer the nails with a hammer. (in Polish, there's no "with")
					Rysuję czym? Rysuję ołówkiem.	I paint with what? I paint with a pencil.
					Jadę czym? Jadę pociągiem. 	I go by what? I go by train.

				Describes what you're interested about:

					Interesuję się czym? Interesuję się samochodami.	I'm into what? I'm into cars.
					Interesuję się czym? Interesuję się gotowaniem.		I'm into what? I'm into cooking.

				Describes who you are:

					Jestem kim? Jestem Polakiem.		I'm who? I'm Polish.
					Jesteś kim? Jesteś nauczycielem.	You are who? You are a teacher.
					On jest kim? On jest więźniem.		He is who? He is a prisoner.

			- Narzednik words end in:
							Singular masculin:	-em
							Singular feminin:	-ą
							Plural:			-ami

	Miejscownik	- Used to mainly describe a place, location (as the name Latin says, Locativus - location)
			- Should be used with a preposition of location(when having a static meaning):
							w	(in)	I work where? I work in London - Pracuję gdzie? Pracuję w Londynie.
									I work in what city? I work in London - Pracuję w jakim mieście? Pracuję w Londynie.
									I left it (in) where? I left it in the fridge - Zostawiłem to w czym?(gdzie) Zostawiłem to w lodówce.
							na	(on)	I am sitting on what? I am sitting on a bench - Siedzę na czym? Siedzę na ławce.
			- When you think about something.
							I think about who? I think about you - Myślę o kim? Myślę o tobie.
							I think about what? I think about China - Myślę o czym? Myślę o Chinach.

	Wołacz		- Old school way to address someone. Nowadays surpassed by Mianownik.
			
				"Litwo! Ojczyzno moja! ty jesteś jak zdrowie;
				 Ile cię trzeba cenić, ten tylko się dowie,
				 Kto cię stracił.
								~Adam Mickiewicz

				"Lithuania! my fatherland! You are like health;
				 How much you must be valued, will only discover
				 The one who has lost you."
						(translation by Katie Busch-Sorensen)

				In this case, Litwa(Lithuania) and ojczyzna(fatherland) is used in a form of wołacz. Adam mickiewicz addresses Lithuania while saying all the words.
